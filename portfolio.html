Student Publications — Reaching North America

Channel

Student Publications

Duration

2 weeks live

Impressions

65,000+

Overview

The brief

Nearing the end of 2025, stakeholders from the university's Faculty of Law expressed interest in expanding the faculty's presence in North America for their flagship post-graduate courses. With a limited end-of-year budget, I proposed a small-scale online marketing campaign as an experiment to test the viability of using student publications as a targeted channel overseas.

⬅ Add screenshot of banner on student publication website

Rationale

Why student publications

01

Student publications primarily serve one demographic — university students — who are the exact target audience for post-graduate course offerings.

02

Publications are university-specific, allowing for targeting of high-intent geographical areas and the optimisation of creative messaging to a relevant audience.

03

Online delivery through both websites and newsletters allows for flexible scheduling, budget-specific campaigns, and A/B testing — all ideal for an experimental campaign with a smaller budget.

Research

Building the candidate list

I coordinated the campaign from ideation to execution and metrics collection. Using admissions data collected by our university, I identified and synthesised a list of North American colleges with the highest admissions — indicative of pre-existing student interest. After inquiring about advertising rates from all potential publications, I cross-referenced pricing and potential reach to select the final candidates.

From this, I presented a detailed proposal to the faculty stakeholders, outlining the preferred candidates and possible messaging and copy examples.

Execution

Running the campaign

I negotiated rates with each publication's advertising team and outlined a marketing plan, splitting the budget 70/30 between web banners and newsletters. Leveraging a pre-existing cross-university partnership program, I selected a relevant landing page, synthesised custom UTMs, and wrote copy for the creatives. I then liaised with the internal design team to produce two creatives for use across both website and newsletter placements.

Results

What we learned

Over two weeks, the campaign delivered over 65,000 impressions, primarily from the website leaderboard. However, the most valuable finding was the performance gap between formats: with the same creative, newsletter ads achieved a CTR 36× higher than web banners, at a CPC 8× lower.

We also noticed that across three consecutive newsletter sends, clicks decreased significantly on the second and third deliveries — indicating audience saturation from sends placed too closely together. Overall, the campaign achieved its goal of generating measurable reach in a new market while yielding actionable insights for future campaigns.

Collateral Asset Refresh for 2026 Curriculum Change

Assets Updated

48+

Timeline

~6 weeks

Event

Student Recruitment Day

Overview

The brief

In July 2025, FBE finalised significant curriculum changes to their flagship degree, the Bachelor of Commerce. I was tasked with coordinating an urgent project to update all collateral assets to reflect these changes before the university's largest yearly student recruitment event in mid-August 2025.

Scope

What needed updating

01

A 32-page course brochure

02

9 different course structure posters

03

7 major-specific web pages

⬅ Add screenshot of a poster and/or a web page

Execution

Coordinating through the chaos

As the curriculum changes were recent, many stakeholders within the faculty were still unclear on its details. This led to conflicting editorial requests, confusing comments, and inaccurate feedback on mockups. To efficiently coordinate these efforts, I set out to become well-versed in all the changes being made, minimising confusion when liaising with stakeholders and the team.

By consulting colleagues directly involved with the curriculum, I synthesised a Single Source of Truth (SSoT) document to inform all collateral changes. From this, I organised cleanly annotated copies of each asset — incorporating relevant stakeholder feedback — and sent them to both our internal design team and CMS specialist. After many rounds of draft revision with the design team and faculty, I distributed the final mockups to all relevant stakeholders before printing submission.

Results

Delivered on time

All assets were updated accurately and delivered to the faculty's student recruitment team prior to the event, where they were showcased and handed out to prospective students. Given the extremely short timeline and the volume of conflicting stakeholder input, the outcome was deemed satisfactory by all stakeholders involved.

Overview

The brief

At an internal knowledge-sharing session at FBE, I presented data-informed insights on the effects of generative AI on user behaviours and search engine marketing. The goal was to garner internal interest and attention toward GenAI search and underscore the need for strategic adaptation of current marketing activities during a period of significant industry-wide landscape shift.

Content

What I covered

Utilising user behaviour data and metrics collected from our team's marketing campaigns — alongside materials from Google's own documentation and third-party research — the presentation covered the state of online search, recent changes to search engines, current effects on user behaviour, observations on marketing metrics, and beneficial approaches and best practices for the team going forward.
